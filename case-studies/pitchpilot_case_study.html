The Problem
Cold email works. Scaling it doesn't.

A resume on LinkedIn is one of 400. A cold email to a founder is one of maybe five that week. The conversion difference is real — but the reason most people don't do it is time. Researching a company, finding the right angle, writing something that doesn't sound templated — that's 45 minutes per email, done well. Scale that to 20 companies and you've lost a day.

The other problem: personalization breaks at scale. You can write one great email. You can't write twenty with genuine company-specific context without help. This pipeline gives you that help — and keeps you in control of every word that goes out.

Auto Research~2 min
Run python auto_research.py. Scrapes each company website via BeautifulSoup, fetches recent news via Google News RSS + NewsAPI fallback, then sends it all to Groq (LLaMA 3.3) to generate two columns: Why This Company and Your Angle — tailored to your active profile.
Review10 min · your call
Open the Sheet. Read the AI-generated research and angles. Edit anything that looks off. Type Approved in column L for rows you're happy with. Nothing sends without your explicit sign-off — the human gate is the most important part of the system.
Send~1 min
Run python generate_emails.py. Generates a personalized cold email per company using Groq + few-shot examples, sends via Gmail SMTP, BCCs you automatically on every email, updates Status to Sent, logs Date Sent, and writes everything to a CSV outreach log.
Dashboardoptional
Run python dashboard.py to generate a visual HTML dashboard from the CSV log — outreach volume, reply rates, status breakdown. Full pipeline visibility without opening a spreadsheet.

Smart Feature
Three profiles. One switch.

The pipeline carries three distinct sender profiles — each with different skill highlights, project emphasis, and email tone. Change one line in auto_research.py to switch between them. Groq uses the active profile to generate your angle and tailor the email accordingly.

Design Decisions
Why these choices were deliberate.

Google Sheets over a database: The tracker needed to be human-readable, editable on mobile, and shareable without setup. Sheets is the perfect interface for a human-in-the-loop system — everyone already knows how to use it.

Few-shot prompting for email generation: Zero-shot emails sounded robotic. Adding real cold email examples to the prompt — not instructions, but actual examples — transformed output quality. The model learned tone from evidence, not rules.

Auto BCC on every send: Every email that goes out lands in your own inbox too. You always know exactly what was sent, to whom, with what wording. No surprises.

NewsAPI as fallback to Google News RSS: Google News RSS works well for larger companies. Smaller startups often have poor RSS coverage. The fallback dramatically improved research quality for lesser-known targets.

Learnings
What building this actually taught me.

Few-shot prompting is the single highest-leverage improvement you can make to LLM output quality. Better than temperature tuning, better than longer system prompts.

Human-in-the-loop isn't a workaround — it's a product principle. The approval gate keeps quality high and builds trust in the system. Automation without oversight is just spam at scale.

Secrets management is non-negotiable from day one. Nearly committed .env and credentials.json to GitHub early on. Set up .gitignore before writing the first line of code, not after.

News coverage drops sharply for smaller startups. Fallback layering — RSS → NewsAPI → website scrape — is essential for real-world data quality across targets of all sizes.

The dashboard was underrated. Adding visual outreach stats transformed the system from a script into a tool with feedback loops — you could actually see what was working.

PitchPilot didn't just save time — it changed how I think about automation with intention. The best pipelines don't replace humans. They amplify them.
